list of screws and fasteners

x rail screws

flange head m5 x 40mm screws, passing through the x rail, the x rail angle, and the slate bed.

note that the end four screws (2x2) are m5 x 50mm, because these also need to pass through the x ball screw mounting angle.

gantry side screws

m5 x 40mm flange head screws (x9 for each side)... these are the screws which just pass through the gantry side and 6.35mm aluminium angle.

m6 x 60 flange head screws (x9 for each side)... these are the screws which must pass through the x drive arm, gantry side, and 6.35mm aluminium angle.

y motor mount screws

m6 x 25 flange head screw (x2).

combined with m6 tee-nut on the other side of the x drive arm.

gantry back screws

m6 x 50mm flange head screws (x6 on each side). used with 10mm od dowel nut to affix the gantry back to the gantry sides.

(optional) m5 x 40 flange head screws (x12) fixed with nut/washer for the gantry back stiffener.

ball screw bearing screws

m6 x 70mm socket head (x8 for the bf bearings, and x8 for the bk bearings).

m6 x 70mm countersunk screws (x8 for the x ball screw bk bearings). note that these two bearings need countersunk screws so that their mounting angles can sit flush with the underside of the base.

sbr20uu screws

m6 x 15mm countersunk screws (x4 per bearing x 8 bearings).

sbr16uu screws

m5 x 15mm countersunk screws (x4 per bearing x 4 bearings).

rm1605 ball nut screws

m5 x 20mm socket or flange head screws (x6 per ball nut x 2 ball nuts).

rm2005 ball nut screws

m6 x 20mm socket or flange head screws (x6 per ball nut x 2 ball nuts).

rm1605 ball nut mount screws

z axis: m5 x 30mm flange head screw (x4).

y axis: m5 x 15mm countersunk screw (x4).

rm2005 ball nut mount screws

y axis: m6 x 15mm countersunk screw (x4 per mount x 2 mounts).

y carriage angle screws

m5 x 12mm flange head screws (x10 x2) **attached into threaded holes on the z carriage angles.**

m5 x 40mm flange head screws (x4) fastened with washers/nuts.

spindle mount screws

m5 x 30mm flange head screws (x2), fastened with m5 tee-nuts.

m6 x 50mm (or 40mm) flange head screws (x2). attached in the opposite direction from the top spindle mount screws, with washer/nut.

linear drive - x axis

fitting the x rail to the base

use the pre-drilled sbr20 holes as a guide.

use some spare aluminium angle as a spacer.

use a diamond coated holesaw to make the holes in the slate base. slow and steady wins the day!

drill holes in ballscrew mount (floating end)

bearing block bf12/15 will be aligned to the corner of the angle aluminium.

the inner face of the aluminium angle will be aligned to the end of the table base.

the floating end bearing will be the anchor point to the base/x-rail. the fixed end and nema motor will be a function of the ballscrew length.

drill holes in ballscrew mount (fixed end)

1) align aluminium angle face to nema motor mounting face

2) affix ballscrew into fixed bearing bk15 and mate with nema motor shaft using the appropriate coupler.

3) mark and drill holes to fix the fixed bearing and the nema motor to the aluminium angle

4) ensure ball nut is on the ballscrew shaft, with ball nut mount attached. affix ballscrew into floating bearing, and affix floating bearing onto the aluminium angle

5) mark and drill holes to fix the aluminium angle mount onto the base, using the x rail screws

6) attach entire x ballscrew assembly onto the underside of the base

cut gantry sides

machine the gantry sides out of stable 18mm playwood (or your preferred material) according to the specs in the image above.

repeat for both sides.

cut x drive arm

machine the x drive arms out of stable 18mm playwood (or your preferred material) according to the specs in the image above.

repeat for both sides.

cut gantry back

machine the gantry back out of stable 18mm playwood (or your preferred material) according to the specs in the image above.

repeat for both sides.

attaching the gantry back to the gantry sides

align gantry back with one of the gantry sides such that the gantry back spans the full width.

drill 6mm holes into the gantry back at frequencies specified in the diagram, offset from the edge by half the gantry side thickness.

use a dowelling jig or similar to drill matching holes in the gantry side edges. drill holes in the gantry side faces for the dowel buts to fit into.

attaching gantry side angle

mark and drill holes for the gantry side angle to act as a bracket between the gantry back and gantry sides.

ensure adequate clearance from the various gantry side, y-rail, and y ball screw bearing holes.

Drilling SBRUU mounting holes into gantry side angle

Once the gantry sides/back are attached to the gantry side angle, seat the entire gantry assembly onto the x rail SBRUU bearings, such that it is symetric along the x axis. Ensure the SBRUU's are parallel to the outer back side of the gantry side angle, and mark the location of the bearing blocks. Measure and mark the centre points of the 4 holes (per SBRUU) and drill M6 holes. Countersink on the upper face (the inner, shorter face).

linear drive - y axis

Attach y rails

1) attach the top y rail flush with the top of the gantry back's inner face. tighten screws firmly.

2) align the bottom y rail with the lower insid edge of the gantry back's inner face. holes should be drilled1mm oversize into the gantry back, and at this stage screws should be finger tight.

3) attach and tighten gantry back stiffening angle.

drill holes for y nema motor mount and fixed side ballscrew bearing

1) align fixed side bearing (bk12) of the y ballscrew on the gantry back, flush with the inner gantry side face, and a spacer's width above the y rail.

2) mark and drill 4 mounting holes into the gantry back, and temporarily hold the bearing block in position.

3) align and mark holes in the y motor mounting bracket. to match the nema motor mounting holes, and the bk12 mounting holes.

drill holes for the floating side y ballscrew bearing

1) assemble the bk12, the rm16 ballscrew, the ballnut and ballnut mount, and the bf12 floating side bearing.

2) hold in place, referenced to the freshly drilled bk12 mounting holes, and mark and drill mounting holes for the bf12 floating bearing.

3) mount the y ballscrew assembly and nema motor/mount and tighten screws.

linear drive - z axis

Attach y-axis linear bearings to y-carriage angles

Align the bearings into the inner corners of the y-carriage angles.

Screw heads need to be fully countersunk into y carriage angles.

Attach y-Carriage Angle to Z-Carriage Angle

Start with the lower y-carriage angle. Drill M5 holes into the y-carriage angle first. Then mate up against the z-carriage angle, 31.5mm from the bottom of the z-carriage angle. Double check for square. Drill and tap matching M5 holes into the z-carriage angke. Use M5 x 10mm flange head screws.

Attach both y-carriage angles onto the y-bearings, and hang on the y-rails. Clamp the z-carriage angles onto the y-carriage angles and dril pilot/marking holes.

Dismount the z-carriage and top y-carriage angle and complete the hole drilling. *drill M5 holes into the y-carriage angles, and tap M5 holes into the z-carriage angles.

Attach Linear Rail to z-rail angle

Seat the SBR rail along the inside corner of the z-carriage angles, using a spare 6.35mm angle as a spacer.

Drill exact holes only on one side - the wider aluminium angle.

Drill oversize holes in the other angle, to allow for alignment later.

setup notes for electronics - notably the rpi/arduino interfaces, and the bcnc software setup.

Raspberry Pi Setup

Raspberry Pi intro

the brilliant raspberry pi (rpi) is really at the heart of controlling the cnc machine. it runs bcnc, the software which processes the gcode and talks directly to the arduino which runs grbl and drives the motors. furthermore, the setup is designed from the ground up to run "headless" - rpi talk for not having any connected monitor, keyboard or mouse. instead, the rpi is itself controlled "remotely" by another computer on the network, or even another computer anywhere in the world connected via the internet. what this means in practice is that a designer can edit their design and generate the gcode for the cam toolpaths from their home pc, and then "remote in" to the rpi in their garage (or anywhere in the world) to start the routing. internet of things, indeed! of course, with power comes responsibility, and there are legitimate security concerns that need resolving.

Raspbian Image Install

Raspbian is the Linux operating system that we will be installing onto our RPi. To do this, we need to download an "image" onto an SD card using our regular PC. The image is how we initally boot and load the starting software packages onto our RPi. The official documentation for installing images can be found here.

To help you out, we have already got a pre-made image with everything you need. DOWNLOAD THIS , and burn the image to a micro SD card, following the instructions for Windows, Mac OS, or Linux as appropriate.

Step 1: Download Raspbian/CNC Image

The guys at protoneer.co.nz have kindly put together a pre-configured Raspberry Pi image with everything you need to get started. Download the image here.

Step 2: Format the SD Card

The SD card needs formatting to ensure is is empty and clean. Download SD Formatter from here and follow these instructions.

To be honest, there really isn't much to it - just pop the SD card into your PC, make a note of what drive it appears in, open SD Formatter and check that the right drive is selected, and then hit "Format", clicking through the warnings.

Step 3: Burn the Image to the SD Card

Once the SD card is formatted and ready to be written, the Raspbian/CNC image is ready to be burned to it. For Windows PCs download Win32 Disk Imager here and follow these instructions.

Again, it's easy... open up Win32DiskImager, browse to the above image file you downloaded, and then hit "Write". It'll take a few minutes to write, but then you're done!

Step 4: Boot up the Raspberry Pi!

Now the exciting part - you are ready to breathe life into your Raspberry Pi! Pop the Micro SD card into the slot on the RPi (it's on the underside, at the end opposite the SD slots), then plug in a keyboard/mouse, and hook up the Pi to an HDMI monitor. Then, just plug her in.

You'll see a few lines of text whizz by in the command line shell, but within a few seconds it'll boot straight up into the graphical Operating System. You are up and running!

Initial RPi Configuration (IMPORTANT)

You are now up and running. However, before diving into anything, there are one or two important steps that need to be taken. Simple, but important.

Start by navigating through to Menu->Preferences->Raspberry Pi Configuration

Step 1: CHANGE YOUR PASSWORD!!!!!

The Pi comes shipped with the default password "raspberry", which obviously isn't the most secure password. Before you do anything else, on the System tab of the Configuration screen, press the button to change your password.

Step 2: Expand Filesystem

Press the "Expand Filesystem" button. This ensures that all of your SD card's storage is made available to the RPi.

Step 3: Ensure SSH is enabled

On the "Interfaces" tab, make sure SSH has been enabled.

Step 4: Reboot

When you close the Configuration screen, you should be prompted to reboot the RPi - do this.

Step 5: Connect to Your Network and note down your IP address

Towards the top right of your desktop you will see a network connection icon. Click it, and connect to your network.

Once connected, find your RPi's IP address by opening the Terminal (it is pinned to the bar at the top of the desktop), typing "ifconfig", and hitting enter. Your IP address will be labelled "inet addrs:" next to your relevant connection (wlan0 for wifi, or eth0 if you are connected to ethernet).

TeamViewer Setup

TeamViewer is one of the largest providers of Remote Software in the world. There are lots of choices out there when it comes to remoting in to you RPi, but we support TeamViewer for a number of reasons: 1) Security - TeamViewer features strong encryption, end-to-end encoding, optional two-factor-authentication. 2) Ease of use - No pesky port forwarding, no need to generate static IP addresses. Just install the TeamViewer Host on the RPi and it's good to go. 3) Multi-platform - you can access your RPi from Windows, Mac, Linux, Chrome OS, Android, iOS, Windows App and Blackberry!

Step 1: Download and Open TeamViewer on the RPi

TeamViewer's Host App beta is designed exclusively for running on Raspbian. Download here. This will save into the pi/Downloads folder of the RPi.

Open the downloaded file. You will probably be prompted for your system password.

Step 2: Decide Whether to Run your Pi Headlessly

Running the RPi "headless" means that you will have no monitor/keyboard/mouse plugged in. To enable this you must set up a TeamViewer password for unattended access. In the Rpi right click the TeamViewer icon in the top right of the desktop and select Options. Under "Security" enter a secure password under "Personal password (for unattended access)". Now, the RPi is accessible even in headless mode using this password.

If you prefer not to make unatteded access possible, then stick with the default, setting, whereby you let the RPi's TeamViewer host generate a random password, which you then communicate to the remote user. We'd recommend increasing the password strength in the Options->Security tab.

Remote Desktop Setup

RDP is Microsoft's answer to remote working, and works by entering a computer address into a remote desktop connection screen, and then entering the user credentials of the target computer. In our case, the RPi image was already pre-loaded with XRDB, which makes this easy for us.

While the RPi is on, simply open up the Remote Desktop Connection Windows app, enter the RPi's IP address, and then the pi username/password when prompted. That's it! You'll have the RPi gui open up in a new window, as if you were working directly on the pi itself.

One note... by default, this will only work when connecting in from another PC on the same LAN.

SSH Setup

ssh (secure shell) is a protocol for securely granting access to remote computers. it is encrypted, and is what we will use to "tunnel" through a "vnc" connection (see below) to ensure that everything is secure. ssh itself can be used to remote in to the rpi, but is only recommended for those who are comfortable controlling a computer via a terminal screen. for our purposes, it is a means to an end, and can be set up as per these instructions.

note that as of november 2016 the raspbian with pixel image available from reapberrypi.org downloads has changed a setting in order to disable ssh by default. now, for new users who wish to set up their new raspberry pi headlessly they first need to use any computer with and sd card to add a file called "ssh" into the /boot/ directory. now, when the image is loaded, ssh will be enabled, and allow headless operation. of course, enabling ssh also potentially opens the pi up to the internet, so it is important to change the password for the pi. see this update for more details.

to set up your rpi headlessly, follow the steps as laid out here.

VNC Setup

vnc (virtual network computing) is the protocol that allows us to remotely control the graphical desktop of the raspberry pi. you can use any computer in the world to vnc into your raspberry pi, and it will be as though you are working directly on the raspberry pi itself. however, there are security concerns with directly vnc-ing in because once connected the data exchanged are not encrypted. therefore, we will set up the vnc to "tunnel" through the ssh connection we created above.

the first step is to setup the vnc "server" on the rpi itself. we might interchangeably use the terms server/host/remote to refer to the rpi in this context. follow the instructions here upto, but not including the section titled "connecting to your rraspberry pi with vnc viewer". that link refers to the vnc server provided by realvnc. a popular alternative is to use tightvncserver on the raspberry pi - click here for instructions.

once the vnc server is set up, we need to think about how you access it from your local computer (the one that you are physically working on). this might be a computer on the same home network as the rpi, or, since our machine has been designed to be fully web-connected, this computer can actually be any internet-connected computer (or tablet, or even smartphone!) in the world.

the way we set this up is by using remot3.it, by weaved inc.the remot3.it software is installed onto the rpi and can be used to manage just this pi or a large number of pis, from the internet. this can be used to access and tcp port on the pi over the web including ssh, vnc, http(s), rdp and custom tcp services. in our case, we will be using remot3.it to access the pi via vnc - although note that remot3.it just sets up the connection and generates ip addresses/ports to connect, using your own vnc client/viewer. for more details on installing remot3.it see the raspberrypi.org website, and for full instructions see the weaved.com instructions here and here.

it's worth mentioning one other service, teamviewer, that looks very promising for our application. teamviewer is fully secure, and appears to be much more user friendly and easy to deploy. see their website for more details, or see here for a good example of how teamviewer was used to control a raspberry pi home surveillance system.

GRBL Setup

GRBL Intro

GRBL is a totally free, open source CNC milling controller. It has been designed to run on a simple Arduino. It takes the G-code generated by the CAD/CAM software, and talks to the motors, spindles, coolant, and all the other real-world stuff that actually make the part.

Flashing GRBL to the Arduino

Once again the guys at protoneer.co.nz have made our lives easier. This time, they have created a GRBL Arduinio Library, which allow you to use the Arduino IDE to flash GRBL to the Arduino, instead of making and loading HEX files onto the board. See their site for full instructions, which we'll repeat, below:

Step 1: Download the library from Github https://github.com/Protoneer/GRBL-Arduino-Library/archive/master.zip

Step 2: Unzip the library and copy the main folder into the Libraries folder in the folder you installed your Arduino software. Eg. C:\arduino-1.0.3\libraries\

Step 3: Rename the folder to GRBL. (This will stop the Arduino IDE from complaining about the long folder name)

Step 4: Open up the Arduino IDE.

Step 5: Click on the following menu : File -> Examples -> GRBL (or what ever you renamed the folder to) -> ArduinoUno

Step 6: Upload the sketch to your Arduino UNO board.

grbl steps/mm settings

rm1605 and rm2005 have a 5mm lead. this means they produce linear travel of 5mm per turn of the ball screw. the gcode settings which relate directly to this are $100 (x steps/mm), $101 (y, steps/mm), $102 (z, steps/mm). the final setting will also depend on how much microsetting there is.

for example, a 5mm lead on a ball screw means that one complete revolution of the ball screw moves the ballnut 5mm. if the stepper motor has 200 steps per revolution (1.8 degrees per step), then for a 5mm lead ball screw, that implies a gcode setting of 200/5 = 40 steps/mm. that implies a resolution of 1/40 = 0.025 mm.

for further details on how to set up the cnc shield for microstepping, see the protoneer blog.
